Memory & Time · Essay No. 01

The Past Is Not a Place: Tarkovsky and the Archaeology of Memory

What does it mean to return somewhere that exists only inside you? Andrei Tarkovsky's late films are not stories about the past — they are the past, materialised and inaccessible at once.

By Elara Voss March 2026 18 min read

There is a scene in Mirror — Andrei Tarkovsky's most autobiographical film — in which a woman stands in a field as the wind moves through the long grass around her. Nothing happens, precisely. And yet everything happens. She does not turn around. The camera does not cut. Time distends, folds over itself, becomes something other than a vessel for plot.

To watch Tarkovsky is to confront a cinema that refuses to explain itself. His films do not offer the satisfaction of resolution, nor do they reward patience with clarity. They offer something stranger and more profound: the sensation of memory itself — not the narrated past, but the past as a physical presence, as humidity in a room, as a sound heard through a wall.

"He is not filming the past. He is trying to hold it still long enough to understand why it will not stay."

Tarkovsky wrote at length about what he called "sculpting in time" — the idea that cinema's essential material is not light or movement but duration, that a film is an act of preserving time the way an insect is preserved in amber. The metaphor is instructive but also slightly misleading, because amber is static, and the past in Tarkovsky's cinema is anything but.

In Nostalghia, the Russian poet Andrei Gorchakov travels to Italy to research the life of an eighteenth-century serf musician. He is, ostensibly, studying the past. But the film makes clear very quickly that he is being studied by it — that the past is the more active force in this transaction, the one doing the looking. Memory, for Tarkovsky, is not something you visit. It is something that visits you.

The final sequence of The Sacrifice — in which Alexander burns his house to the ground as a bargain with God — is perhaps the most extreme expression of this logic. To destroy the present as an offering to something larger than oneself, something that exists outside ordinary time. It is a mad act and a holy one, and Tarkovsky films it with excruciating patience, the camera refusing to avert its gaze from the catastrophe it has chosen to witness.

In Conversation · April 2026

"Cinema is the only art form that can make you feel time passing"

The director of Hollow Hours and The Cartographer's Wife speaks to Meraki about duration, restraint, and why she stopped watching films for two years.

Interviewer Your last two films have been described as "deliberately slow." Is that something you set out to do, or is it a conclusion critics reach when they don't know what else to say?

I think it's both, honestly. I do make deliberate formal choices about pace — about when to cut, about how long to stay with something. But "slow" as a critical category is almost always a complaint wearing the costume of a description. What it really means is: this film did not give me what I expected, when I expected it. And I'm fine with that. I'm more interested in what happens after the expectation dies.

Interviewer You stopped watching films for two years. What happened during that time, and how did it change how you work?

I was so saturated. I had been watching three, four films a week for fifteen years — festivals, screeners, retrospectives, homework. And at a certain point I realised I couldn't feel them anymore. They were passing through me without leaving a trace. So I stopped. I read novels, I took long walks, I cooked. I became, for the first time in years, an ordinary person with ordinary attention. When I came back to cinema, it felt like returning to something I'd loved as a child — that quality of being seized by something.

Interviewer Hollow Hours has almost no music. Was that always the plan, or did you begin with score and strip it back?

We had a score. A very beautiful one — I worked with a composer I admire enormously. And at some point in the edit, I watched a cut without it by accident. A technical problem. The picture played without music for about twenty minutes, and I realised that the film was louder without it. The sounds of the space — breathing, fabric, distant traffic — were doing something the music had been trying to compensate for. The music was kind, really. It was holding the audience's hand when they needed to find their own way.

Interviewer What do you want from an audience? Not in general — specifically from the person who sits down with one of your films.

I want them to get bored, and then to get curious about why they're bored, and then to stop being bored. There's a particular quality of attention that only appears on the other side of impatience — a kind of sharpened noticing. I'm not making films for the person who arrives already willing. I'm making films for the person who resists, and then gives in. That surrender is what I'm after. Not agreement. Surrender.

Interviewer The title of your new project — can you tell us anything about it?

Almost nothing, which is how I prefer it. I'll say this: it begins with a woman who has forgotten something, and it ends before we find out what. Everything in between is the texture of not-knowing. Whether that's a film or a feeling, I haven't quite decided yet.

N

Nadia Svarvati

Film Director

Nadia Svarvati is a Norwegian-Iranian director whose work explores memory, domestic space, and the politics of attention. Her debut feature Hollow Hours won the FIPRESCI Prize at Locarno; her second, The Cartographer's Wife, was selected for Un Certain Regard. She lives between Oslo and Paris.

A place for people who take cinema seriously — and themselves lightly.

Meraki is an independent film publication dedicated to long-form criticism, close reading, and the kinds of conversations that happen after the lights come on and no one wants to leave.

We believe that a film review is not a verdict. It is an invitation — to look again, to think differently, to notice what you missed the first time. We write slowly, about films both new and old, with as much patience as we ask of our readers.

We have no algorithm, no traffic targets, no sponsored content. We are funded by readers who think good criticism is worth paying for, and by the occasional grant from institutions patient enough to support work that does not optimize for anything in particular.

The word meraki is Greek. It means to do something with soul, with creativity, with love — to put a piece of yourself into what you make. It is what we ask of the filmmakers we write about. It is what we try to bring to every piece we publish.

"We do not write about films. We write because of them — because something in a frame, a cut, a held silence, asked to be thought about more carefully than the night allowed."
